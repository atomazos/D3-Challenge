Health Officials Urge States to Address Link between Income Levels and Obesity Rates

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the American Diabeties Association (ADA) and other health officials are calling for immediate action to stem the tide of the growing obesity epidemic in the country. According to recent data from the US Census Bureau, people in the US who reside in lower income states are more prone to obesity. Mississippi, West Virginia, Arkansas, Tennessee, Kentucky, Louisiana, Oklahoma, Alabama and South Carolina are all in the top 10 poorest and most obese states.

Although health officials caution that income is only one factor related to a state's obesity rate, they highlight that the census data is consistent with many recent studies that reveal a link between poverty and so-called “food deserts” – counties that have poor access to affordable, healthy nutrition. The ADA have proposed that states and cities consider providing tax breaks and other subsidies to grocers and other food purveyors to incentivize them to open stores in impovershied areas.

The American Medical Association (AMA) also emphasize that public recreation awareness and funding can play an important role in chipping away at obesity rates. Jane Doe, Dircetor of the Obesity Action Committee at the AMA, says that “it all comes down to physical activity and nutrition – areas of the country with lower levels of income and higher poverty rates need more parks, bicyle paths, public gyms, as well as more affordable nutritional options and less fast-food restaurants.”
